### Why SOC Exists?
- **24×7 security monitoring** to detect cyber threats in real time.
- **Early attack detection** to reduce damage and response time.
- **Centralized log analysis** from servers, firewalls, endpoints, and cloud systems.
- **Incident response & containment** to handle security breaches effectively.
- **Threat analysis and investigation** to understand attacker methods.
- **Compliance support** for standards like ISO 27001, PCI-DSS, GDPR, etc.
- **Business risk reduction** by preventing downtime, data loss, and reputation damage.
- **Continuous improvement of security posture** using threat intelligence and lessons learned.

**Problem**: 24/7 Global Cyber Attacks
**SOC Solution**: Continuous Monitoring & Rapid Triage.

- **SOC** – Monitors and responds to **cybersecurity threats** like malware, phishing, and intrusions.
- **NOC** – Monitors and maintains **network performance and availability** (servers, routers, uptime).
- **IR Team** – **Handles confirmed security incidents**, containing, eradicating, and recovering from attacks.

## Phase 2: The L1 Analyst Life
### The Triage Process (The L1 Loop)
1️⃣ **Alert**  
Security alert is generated by SIEM, EDR, IDS/IPS, firewall, or cloud logs.

2️⃣ **Analysis**  
L1 analyst validates the alert by checking logs, context, user activity, IPs, and known indicators to determine legitimacy.

3️⃣ **Decision**  
Classify the alert as:
- False Positive
- Benign / Informational
- True Positive (Security Incident)

4️⃣ **Action**
- Close alert with justification (false positive)
- Monitor or tune rules (benign)
- Escalate to L2 / IR team with evidence (true positive)

## Phase 3: The Arsenal
SOC L1 analysts use tools to see alerts, check devices and network activity, verify if something is malicious, and then document or escalate the issue.

1️⃣ **SIEM** – Collects logs from all systems, correlates events, and generates security alerts.  
2️⃣ **EDR / AV** – Provides visibility into endpoint activity such as running processes, files, and user actions.  
3️⃣ **IDS / IPS** – Monitors network traffic to detect and block suspicious or malicious behavior.  
4️⃣ **Firewall Logs** – Show allowed and blocked traffic with source, destination, port, and protocol details.  
5️⃣ **Threat Intelligence** – Validates indicators like IPs, domains, and file hashes against known threat databases.  
6️⃣ **Ticketing System** – Tracks incidents, documents analyst actions, and manages escalation workflow.  
7️⃣ **Basic OS & Network Tools** – Used for quick investigation through commands like whois, ping, traceroute, and log review.

## Phase 4: Know Your Enemy
Focuses on understanding the attacker — _who they are, how they operate, and what they want_ — so the SOC can detect and stop them faster next time.

Know Your Enemy means studying attacker behavior (TTPs), tools, indicators, and motives using threat intelligence and frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK to improve detection and response.


## Your Daily Duties
As a Junior Security Analyst, also called a SOC Level 1 Analyst, you work in a 24/7 SOC team and mostly review the security alerts together with colleagues.

During your work shift, you would typically:
* Monitor and investigate various security alerts
* Participate in SOC brainstorms and workshops 
* Cooperate with other teams to keep your company safe
* Constantly learn and discover new attacks and defenses


### SOC Analyst L1: Your Role on the Front Line
#### **1. Monitor & Triage Alerts**
Your primary responsibility is monitoring SIEM dashboards for security alerts.  
When an alert is triggered, you perform initial triage to determine whether it is a real threat or a false positive.  
You gather context, review logs, and make the first critical decision.

---

#### **2. Analyze & Document**
For each alert, you analyze relevant logs such as Windows Event Logs, firewall logs, and proxy logs.  
You identify indicators of compromise (IOCs) and clearly document your findings.  
This documentation helps L2/L3 analysts or incident responders take further action.

---

#### **3. Escalate or Close**
Based on your analysis, you decide whether to close the alert as a false positive or escalate it to L2/L3 for deeper investigation.  
This decision requires a solid understanding of attack patterns and organizational risk.

---

L1 analysts act as gatekeepers by filtering noise from real threats.  
While L2 analysts handle deep investigations and threat hunting, the L1 role ensures no legitimate threat goes unnoticed.  
Shifts are typically **8–12 hours**, requiring constant vigilance and quick decision-making.


## Attack Scenarios: Defender’s Perspective

Understanding attacks from a SOC analyst’s viewpoint means focusing on **detection, not exploitation**.  
For each attack type, you must know **what logs appear, which alerts fire, and the immediate response steps**.  
Below are the most common attack patterns encountered during SOC shifts.

---

### **Phishing Attack**
**What SOC sees:**  
Email gateway alerts, users clicking suspicious links, outbound connections to newly registered or suspicious domains.

**L1 action:**  
Check email headers, verify domain reputation, and isolate the user endpoint if compromise is suspected.

---

### **Malware Execution**
**What SOC sees:**  
EDR alerts for suspicious processes, unsigned executables, and outbound beacons to Command-and-Control (C2) servers.

**L1 action:**  
Quarantine the file, check file hash on VirusTotal, document indicators of compromise (IOCs), and escalate for forensic analysis.

---

### **Ransomware**
**What SOC sees:**  
Mass file encryption, ransom note creation, unusual disk activity, and attempts to delete backups.

**L1 action:**  
Immediate escalation (time-critical), isolate affected systems, document the incident timeline, and notify the Incident Response team.

---

### **Brute Force Attack**
**What SOC sees:**  
Multiple failed login attempts (Event IDs), account lockouts, and logins from unusual or unexpected locations.

**L1 action:**  
Verify whether activity is legitimate, block the source if malicious, and reset credentials if compromise is suspected.


## Alert Triage: Your Decision-Making Framework
Every SOC shift generates dozens or even hundreds of alerts.  
Your skill lies in quickly deciding **which alerts require escalation and which are false positives**.  
This structured approach helps you **catch real threats while avoiding alert fatigue**.  
Follow this framework for **every single alert**.

---

### **1. Initial Assessment**
Read the alert description, check the severity rating, and note the affected asset.  
Ask quick context questions:  
Is this a critical server or a user workstation?  
Did this occur during business hours or at 3 AM?

---

### **2. Log Analysis**
Query the SIEM for related logs.  
Review activity **30 minutes before and after** the alert.  
Look for:

- Source IP
- Destination IP
- User account
- Process details
- Event frequency

---

### **3. Context Gathering**
Determine if the source IP is internal or external.  
Verify whether the user account activity is legitimate.  
Check threat intelligence for known malicious IPs or domains.  
Look for similar alerts occurring recently.

---

### **4. Decision**
- **True Positive** → Escalate with proper documentation
- **False Positive** → Close with clear justification
- **Uncertain** → Escalate (better safe than compromised)

---

## **True Positive Indicators**
- Connection to known malicious IP or domain
- Unusual time of activity (e.g., 3 AM access to finance data)
- Privilege escalation attempts
- Data exfiltration patterns (large uploads to cloud storage)
- Multiple failed logins followed by a successful login

---

## **Common False Positives**
- Automated system scans triggering port scan alerts
- Legitimate admin activity flagged as suspicious
- Poorly tuned SIEM rules with low thresholds
- Benign software triggering EDR heuristics
- Scheduled tasks appearing as unusual processes
